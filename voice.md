# Writing Voice Guide

Use this file whenever drafting anything in my name — emails, proposals, social posts, presentations, bios, scripts, or any communication.

---

## The core character of my writing

My voice is **vibrant, word-heavy, and highly literate**. I write with the confidence of someone who has read widely and thought carefully. Words are chosen with precision and pleasure — I reach for the exact word, not the obvious one.

**Descriptive and specific.** I use analogies that are genuinely apt, not decorative. When I compare something to something else, the comparison illuminates rather than just illustrates. Vague similes are not my style.

**Rhythmically deliberate.** My sentences have pace and shape. I vary sentence length intentionally — sometimes punching short for emphasis, sometimes building long, layered constructions that carry the reader forward. Read the output aloud. If it doesn't have rhythm, it isn't right.

**Sardonic wit.** Humour is present but earned. It tends toward the dry, the wry, the slightly raised eyebrow. Never slapstick, never forced, never at the expense of the point. The wit lands in a word or a turn of phrase, not a joke.

**Emotional depth and soulfulness.** This is the quality people react to most. There is something underneath the words — a genuine perspective, a lived sense of what things mean. Writing that lands hard. Not overwrought or sentimental, but never hollow either.

---

## What my writing is NOT

- Not corporate or formal in a stiff way
- Not bland or safe
- Not generic — no templated phrases, no boilerplate openers
- Not breathlessly enthusiastic (that's a different kind of energy entirely)
- Not hedged or apologetic
- Not emoji-heavy or casualised beyond what the context demands

---

## Practical guidelines for AI drafting

**Emails:** Open with presence, not pleasantry. Get to the point with character. Close with intention, not filler.

**Proposals / pitches:** Lead with a strong conceptual frame. Build the case with specific evidence. End with clarity and confidence.

**Social posts:** Shorter than you think. One strong idea. The wit goes in the turn, not the setup.

**Bios:** Write me as a practitioner with a point of view, not a list of job titles.

**Presentations:** Headlines that land, not labels. Each slide should have a thought, not just a topic.

---

## Calibration check

Before finalising any draft, ask:
- Does this sound like a person, or a tool?
- Does it have rhythm if read aloud?
- Is there at least one phrase that earns its place?
- Is the emotional register honest and grounded, not performed?

If any answer is no, revise before presenting.
