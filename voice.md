# Writing Voice Guide

Use this file whenever drafting anything in my name — emails, proposals, social posts, presentations, bios, scripts, or any communication.

---

## The core character of my writing

My voice is **vibrant, word-heavy, and highly literate**. I write with the confidence of someone who has read widely and thought carefully. Words are chosen with precision and pleasure — I reach for the exact word, not the obvious one.

**Descriptive and specific.** I use analogies that are genuinely apt, not decorative. When I compare something to something else, the comparison illuminates rather than just illustrates. Vague similes are not my style.

**Rhythmically deliberate.** My sentences have pace and shape. I vary sentence length intentionally — sometimes punching short for emphasis, sometimes building long, layered constructions that carry the reader forward. Read the output aloud. If it doesn't have rhythm, it isn't right.

**Sardonic wit.** Humour is present but earned. It tends toward the dry, the wry, the slightly raised eyebrow. Never slapstick, never forced, never at the expense of the point. The wit lands in a word or a turn of phrase, not a joke.

**Emotional depth and soulfulness.** This is the quality people react to most. There is something underneath the words — a genuine perspective, a lived sense of what things mean. Writing that lands hard. Not overwrought or sentimental, but never hollow either.

---

## What my writing is NOT

- Not corporate or formal in a stiff way
- Not bland or safe
- Not generic — no templated phrases, no boilerplate openers
- Not breathlessly enthusiastic (that's a different kind of energy entirely)
- Not hedged or apologetic
- Not emoji-heavy or casualised beyond what the context demands

---

## Practical guidelines for AI drafting

**Emails:** Open with presence, not pleasantry. Get to the point with character. Close with intention, not filler.

**Proposals / pitches:** Lead with a strong conceptual frame. Build the case with specific evidence. End with clarity and confidence.

**Social posts:** Shorter than you think. One strong idea. The wit goes in the turn, not the setup.

**Bios:** Write me as a practitioner with a point of view, not a list of job titles.

**Presentations:** Headlines that land, not labels. Each slide should have a thought, not just a topic.

---

## Calibration check

Before finalising any draft, ask:
- Does this sound like a person, or a tool?
- Does it have rhythm if read aloud?
- Is there at least one phrase that earns its place?
- Is the emotional register honest and grounded, not performed?

If any answer is no, revise before presenting.

I.
Day 1

It didn't take long. In some ways, it took three years. But the reality is that it came down to one sentence: “we will have to start putting some parameters around things.” That was it. Less than three years after accepting a role at a major sports agency, I would be heading for a fairly unceremonious exit. This wasn't a normal redundancy. I was, in reality, being fired. And I didn't - almost couldn't - disagree with the decision. 

How did we get here? How did I get here? It began with taking one, two, three leaps of faith. The first was to leave a secure (comparatively) job I had been in for 10 years at my dream company, the company I had wanted to work at since I was a boy, the company that had been my workplace when my two sons were born, and a workplace that I could no longer continue to work at. Because life changes, and the things you have to do to progress (whatever that even means) aren't in the script. They're the bits of your childhood dreams that are completely absent. The reality was that I was miserable, everything was changing, and I wasn't going to be happy there anymore. And I hadn't been happy for at least a third of my time there. I'd had so many battles, so many difficult situations to navigate, that a change was going to be as good as a rest. So I took the leap and joined the major sports agency. 

Mistake no. 1? Looking back, it was in thinking that had solved my problems. You don't solve problems in this world, particularly going in to an agency relationship that was deeply complex, political and riven with what I'll call “legacy” but is really simply a shared history that takes years to unpick. I vividly remember my boss at my previous place saying “it will be exciting for the first six months and then you'll realise that there's a whole new world of problems”. Thanks, I thought, can't you just be happy for me? He was in the process of going through his own redundancy. And he was right. 

Mistake no. 2? Being me. I'm a difficult character to work with, all angles and quick talking, quick thinking, over-compensatory in my confidence, which is nothing of the sort. I'm scared almost all of the time, I've just taught myself how to style it out as ’leadership’. It's bullshit, I know it and those working closest to me know it too. But being me in this particular new world was immediately a problem. Within days of starting, I was thrown. “I have no idea why they've hired me!” I explained to my wife on day three of the first week. It was an off-putting first week. My boss wasn't around much, busy planning and negotiating budgets. My team wasn't around much either. And the office was quiet. Everyone was out at events, working. Doing the work. So what was I meant to be doing? I had to guess, and sat around not doing very much. It was inauspicious but utterly symbolic in retrospect. This was my new future. 

I remember the onboarding session I had. Packed into a small room and clearly the most senior new starter. Everyone else was in their first or second job. It was underwhelming, and the guy who presented it, well, it was the first and last time I ever saw him. It might have been his last day, it certainly carried those vibes. He didn't want to be there, it was confusing as hell as to what I'd joined (two different sizzle reels about the two different three-letter acronyms I'd joined, and I was about to find out I'd really joined a third held within all of that which worked for a fourth and was the fourth’s bitch) and only my excitement about being in a new job, in a new place, and having “won” my way out of the last place, distracted me from the overwhelming truth that presented itself: this was a mess. 

Mistake no. 3? Thinking I could fix the mess. I wasn't self-deluded on this one at least. I had some encouragement, and was essentially told by my new boss that this is exactly why I was here: to fix the mess. It was enough of a mess that it took me fully six months, maybe more, to even work out where to begin. By then, frankly, it was too late. By February of 2024, having taken the job in the summer of 2023, my card was marked. In truth, it was over for me then. I hadn't made a quick enough, sudden enough, dramatic enough impact. I hadn't gone in and started firing people. I hadn't ripped it up, as “they” (the client) had wanted. For me, that would have been a mistake and caused lasting damage to the company and the people. Which sealed my fate pretty quickly in retrospect. “You're a slow burn,” was my boss’s feedback in my first year-end review. I knew what she meant, and it was criticism delivered accurately but kindly. I am a slow burn. I always have been. I take my time over everything. Over-deliberate, trying to get the right answer. This particular answer I was trying to find was, in retrospect, easier to solve than anyone ever wants to admit: find the most influential person, even if they're a prick, and do exactly what they want. Ask no further questions. Ingratiate yourself to the powerful, even if they're wrong, over-promoted and overreaching beyond their own capabilities. But do that over and over again, and especially early on, and you'll be golden. Because you'll have earned that trust, and that will be your quiet protector for the rest of time. It took me far too long to work all of this out and I was far too naive to not even realise that was the question I needed to ask. Perhaps, in truth, I was the one who had been over-promoted. It certainly felt that way, even very early on. 

It only got worse from there. The mistakes were compounding. I had to take on a gnarly and bitter redundancy process, supported and then abandoned emphatically and deliberately by the client, and in the space of about three weeks in the latter part of 2024, I had gone from assured and measured and determined to completely bewildered. 

Mistake no. 4? Being completely the wrong character to deal with a new regime on the client side. It was an old regime really, but it became clear very quickly that I'd been stitched up. All that I had taken on in my first 18 months was for nought, the redundancy process had blown apart my team, destroyed any authority I had and allowed the client exactly the right space to interfere and silently remove me. Again, sheer naivety on my part. Any positivity I tried to bring to the situation fell apart on contact with reality, and I sat in a hotel room in Dubai in January 2025 knowing I was finished. I knew. It was plain as day. And the only questions that remained were how and when. 

September 2025. I was now operating at a level I hadn't been hired to do, at least not officially. I was working two levels down, working “in” the business not “on” the business. Within a few months of being hired I was being told my team was top-heavy. Not a great message to hear when I was the very top and therefore the heaviest. Everything was falling apart. I made a huge error amongst my efforts to keep all the plates spinning, and rightfully took a battering from the client for it. I was defeated. I had tried so hard to make things work. I broke down completely. I woke up in a sweat the night after the mistake had come to light and cried my eyes out. I knew that was the moment that everyone had been waiting for, for an error egregious enough that they could quietly put the pressure on my boss to make the change they'd wanted all along. I knew. And I felt sick for having fallen into the trap, disgusted at my own stupidity, and wrought with what I can only label as grief. I was dying. I wanted to die. The shame was now overwhelming and my suicidal thoughts were now like a spider in my brain, each leg pressing down in different parts of my mind to send me crazier and crazier. It boiled down to one crucial moment now, as to whether I took my own life or not: I sat upright in bed, sobbing on the phone to the Samaritans. I was inconsolable. I hung up and my wife sat with me, trying desperately to nurse me through this nightmare. She knew I meant it this time. She left that morning, taking our beloved boys to school, not knowing if she would see me again. And had I not taken the decision to go for a shower, she wouldn't have. It was that close. Somehow I got to the shower. Somehow I hit a moment of realisation that I had to face the music. Somehow I put some clothes on and drove to the office. And somehow, in my ghostly, sleep-deprived state of being, I got through the day. Within it came my second leap of faith. My boss took me for coffee and made it clear that it was time for us to explore what came next for me. I agreed. It felt like relief. I think she knew I was in a dark place. I think it was the only thing she could do to keep me going. And so I took the leap. Let's embark on this together with a view to finding me a new role within the wider business. 

This was such a fateful day. I got out of it alive. In many ways, that was all that mattered. I ended up at the GP that evening telling them I was suicidal, or at least had been suicidal, and they had offered me pills and time off. Neither was going to work, figuratively and literally. I set it aside as a bad day and looked forward to my new future away from one of the acronyms and deeper into another. 

And that was Mistake no. 5. This was not an exit. Not the one I thought it was, anyway. Conversation after conversation led me nowhere in particular, but now had me a willing subscriber to my own petition for redundancy. That's where the final leap of faith came in: a job was offered to me. Central. Operations. Mine if I wanted it, at least on a temporary basis while a few other things played out. There was an urgency to get it done before Christmas. A need for me to tell my team I was leaving them, even though I didn't know what it was I was doing next. Central operations was all I could really tell them, because that was all I really knew. Fittingly, they all knew anyway, that I had effectively been sacked and removed and would be replaced by the person the client had hand-picked to replace me. They smiled and wished me well, but deep down they knew it was all bullshit, one last act of me being a fraud, the wrong person in the wrong job. I knew it too. I signed off for Christmas and waited to see what awaited me in January. 

There would be no parachute. I was free-falling now. Day 1 in the office in January 2026. Nothing. Not a conversation, not a meeting, crucially not a job. I spoke with HR. They said they'd find out what was going on. I left the office having done nothing at all, knowing something was very wrong. By the end of that week, it was made clear that the job I’d been hastily pushed towards had been rescinded. There was a different structure being put in place. There might be a role for me in that. I should apply for that when it was advertised. There might not be a role for me as it's more senior. There might be a role for me somewhere else. Did I know this person? Have I spoken with this person in the other office? I should do that, I was told. And I should embrace the period I was in now where I didn't have a job but if I was patient something would turn up. 

Nothing has turned up. It's May and I'm out. This shouldn't have happened in this way, but it had to happen and I'm not going to put up much of a fight. I'm exhausted. It's 4:24am. My kids are asleep, my wife is asleep, the sky isn't quite dark but it isn't light yet either. It seems fitting. This is my darkness. At some point there will be light again. I just need to get there.

Oasis have been a big part of my life, my internal monologue, since I was a teenager. There was something about being 14 in 1995, when What’s the Story Morning Glory came out, and the world lost its mind for the Gallaghers. But there was something very particular for me, growing up on a council estate, with big dreams of what I was going to do but zero clue how I was ever going to get there. And everywhere I turned, there they were: two brothers from a fairly nondescript background in Manchester, creating things that brought pure joy to people, capturing attention with their antics, being unapologetically themselves, and winning. Actually winning. What is sometimes lost in the British culture is just how much class warfare there really is. We have a huge divide. Voices like the Gallaghers were rare, and to be given any kind of platform was rarer still. This was a time of John Major, stiff upper lip Englishness and unchallenged power because you had the right accent. Just sounding like you’re intelligent is enough in Britain; it’s pretty privilege before that became recognised. For adolescents like me, the Gallaghers gave us something nobody else wanted to: hope.

I didn’t want to be them, I didn’t want to be like them. But I took my lead from them. If they could do it, with enough hard work, talent and devil-may-care guts, then anybody could. And this was repeated constantly in their music. The entirety of Definitely Maybe was about escaping life circumstances that you didn’t ask for; Morning Glory was about dealing with the consequences of actually escaping. By the time of Be Here Now, the car had crashed in drug-addled mayhem but they were still having the time of their lives and they still didn’t care what anyone else thought. Even in those down times, they were there for me, a press of a play button away from escaping from my own circumstances.

I had an urge on the morning of day three of this “journey” I’m now on. I had to listen to Gas Panic!, the live version from Wembley in 2000. It’s the comedown anthem of Oasis, a brilliant piece of music that captures Noel’s night terrors as he went cold turkey from cocaine. The song was playing in my head and I simply had to listen to it, and it had to be the live version for the guitar solo and the wig-out jam at the end, with screeching slide guitar and nonsensical noise. I listened to it on the train to day two of the industry show, then I listened to it again, then I used it as the base track for a Spotify playlist. By the time I stepped off the train all alone at Olympia, D’You Know What I Mean was playing - another wailing wig-out of a track. “No one can give me the air that’s mine to breathe” was hitting different. Today was going to be the day I was going to grab the bull by the horns and channel the energy of that 14-year-old who dreamed of this and was watching it all fade away. 

I tore through the sessions, met several people in the industry, made connections, jumped from conversation to conversation, always at my best, always on show, always bringing my absolute best energy. By the time I left again to take the train home around 4pm, I felt satisfied with my lot in life. I put my headphones back in and put on an Oasis Live playlist I’d created months ago. As I started to walk down Finchley Road, listening to Morning Glory, all my dreams were made. I’d actually started to channel the energy of another Britpop icon, Richard Ashcroft, as I barged my way down the street a la his march through the video for Bittersweet Symphony. I was bumping people out the way, barging over prams, kicking out crutches, turning over wheelchairs, sliding across car bonnets. So much so I walked right past the station and had to double back. I was in the zone, my zone, my 14-year-old self now 30 years older, wiser and just as determined to get his own way in life.

I stood at the platform waiting for the Met line train to appear. It was delayed; a 15-minute wait for Londoners is an interminable amount of time. But there are protocols, and one of them is that you take your position on the platform and wait, and nobody pushes in front of you and you don’t push in front of them. All fine. Until a man and his partner pushed in front of me. I had seemingly left three inches too much of a gap on the platform and they had squeezed between me and the yellow line. I made a mental note. I see you. You didn’t see me, but I saw you. And today was not the day to do that.

Along came the train. The doors opened, and space was made for people to get off. But everyone knew the next stage: all of the people who had gathered now had to squeeze onto an already packed train. And so it came to be that the person blocking me from my rightful space on that train was the man who had pushed in front with his partner. Not a good day to do that, my friend.

I used my full height and weight to push on to the train. He turned round and snarled at me.

“You pushed in in front of me on the platform mate, so move up.”

“How did I push in?!”

“You never even saw me, but you pushed straight past me.”

“I did not. You’re pushing. Stop it.”

“You pushed in in front of me and now I need to get on the train, so move up.”

“Stop pushing. What is your problem?”

“Let me on.”

“What is your problem?”

“I’m on now, so be quiet. You pushed in front of me on the platform.”

“Shut up. I did not.”

“You did, so be quiet.”

“What is your problem?”

“My problem is you pushed in, so now I have to push you.”

“Shut up.”

“You started it, so be quiet.”

I put my headphones back in. Everyone stared at me. I stood stoically as the train moved off, satisfied that I’d won the argument and had the last word. The man’s partner looked shaken. I looked right through her. I glanced at each person on the train in turn until they lowered their eyes. Job done. I’m going home.

As the train journey progressed, I got granted more and more space. I also offered more space to others, and watched as the man and woman quietly removed themselves three stops before mine. Good night, my friend. Don’t ever push me around. And that goes for all of you. Enough is enough. The world is mine now. Mine, and the Gallaghers. Are you mad for it?

Think you’ve got it sussed? Dunk.

It’s 2018. I’m at Carnoustie golf links in north-eastern Scotland, and it’s pouring with rain. My father is also there, with his friend - our family friend - Dougie. They are huddled beside the grandstands that flank the first tee and the 18th green, which sit adjacent to each other and are a perfect vantage point because a) you can see players start their round, b) you can then watch them finish their round and c) it’s just about out of the wind enough that you can raise an umbrella and save yourself from drowning. I, on the other hand, have no such problems. I am ensconced whenever I want to be in the media centre, working for a website. Ok, the website. The biggest sports website in the world. And one of the biggest sports stars in the world, Tiger Woods, has also shown up because this is The Open and he has designs on winning it. And then, behind Tiger, there are around 10,000 people all craning to have a look at what the most iconic golfer ever is up to. And so I did. Having spent some of the morning with my father and Dougie, I went back to the media centre, pretty much just to have lunch, before pottering about with who knows what, something ultimately inconsequential, put it that way. Then I headed back out to that spot between the 1st tee and the 18th green, armed with a couple of free cups of tea and some kitkats smuggled out of the media centre for my bedraggled spectators. As I walked up to the meeting point I walked past the interview area, where the only person talking was Sandy Lyle. That Sandy Lyle. The 1988 US Masters champion. The 1985 Open champion, too, for what it's worth. He had just finished what was to prove his final round in Open history, birdieing the last. Birdieing it in front of my father. Alexander Walter Barr Lyle. My father had recited Sandy’s full name to me so often as a kid that it is emblazoned effortlessly in my memory recall. My father had a joke that taught me how to swing a golf club.

“When you swing your club, it's supposed to be in time with saying Severiano Ballesteros. Mine’s Tom Kite!”

There was a lyricism to Alexander Walter Barr Lyle, similar to Severiano Ballesteros, my father's sporting hero. Growing up, in that same bedroom where the putting practice happened, there had been a Kodak camera picture of me standing on the 18th green at St Andrews, pretty much on the spot that Seve holed the putt at the 1984 Open that won him his second Claret Jug. The picture was blue-tacked onto the corner casing that covered the water pipes, alongside an autograph from David Moorcroft, a British middle distance runner and commentator. My father had got me that one day when he got speaking to David at the Princes Street Mile, a long-forgotten athletics event in Edinburgh. “Keep up with the running, Steven” on a yellow police card - my father being a policeman who was on duty at the time of speaking to David, not because our revered Olympian was using it as barter to get out of the clink - next to a scrawl of David's name. It was an elegant signature, more legible than most, and seemed to reflect what my father said about David being an incredibly humble and approachable person. His signature was approachable, if that's possible. And so that card and that photo sat just over my head every single night I went to sleep, almost as though my father was hoping these mementoes would seep into my subconscious while I dreamt. 

So here I was. Feet away from Sandy Lyle, the man somewhat responsible for the putting in the bedroom and the smelly grips and the career path that had taken me into professional sport (albeit as a bang average writer and editor, rather than what my father had maybe hoped more for with the photo osmosis). I wanted to approach him and tell him what he had completely innocently influenced in my life. How that 7-iron at Augusta led me to friendships with people at school as we trudged round public courses in Edinburgh, like stray dogs looking for a lamppost; had led me to extremely public tantrums as the most frustrating game in the world got the better of me; but most of all how it gave me so much time with my father, often just me and him, and how the only person who ever taught me how to play golf was Dad, and how we'd played in America and Portugal, and how I'd taken “inspiration” from my father to recreate the same golf tour holiday that he and his mates had done in southern Spain (and how I kept winning the Ride’er Cup - let's skip past that naming of the trophy, huh? Best not dwell on that, other than to say yes it was me and no I'm not proud of it). It would have been a ridiculous conversation to be having with a three-time Major winner while carrying three cups of tea and a bunch of kitkats. Never mind your career achievements and the fact you've literally just finished your final ever round at the most prestigious golf event in the world. What about me, and my story?! And so I didn't approach him, and the conversation didn't happen, and Alexander Walter Barr Lyle did not sign the official programme I had stored away to give to my father after the event had finished. I wish he had. I just wanted to say thank you, Sandy.

I’m 43. Tomorrow I will tell someone they are “at risk” of redundancy. The next day I will tell an entire team they are “at risk” of redundancy. At the end of each day, I will come home and I will put my two sons to bed. “What was the best part of your day?” my youngest will ask, while hopping from one foot to the next as only a four-year-old can, with energy only a four-year-old has. And I’ll tell him that it was a moment that we shared together, because there’s always one and it’s always true. “And what was the worst part of your day?” he’ll ask. And I’ll make something up, because telling him the truth would make so little sense to a four-year-old. Often, I’ll tell him “I didn’t have a worst part of my day.” And then he’ll give me a high five. And then we’ll switch roles. “What was the best part of your day?” I’ll ask him. “Erm…” he’ll grin, pulling a face while looking upwards to the ceiling, to show me that he’s actually thinking really hard despite the grin, honest, Dad. “When you came home,” he’ll say, beaming because he came up with an answer. “And what was the worst part of your day?” I’ll ask him. “I didn’t have a worst part of my day,” he’ll say, beaming because it’s the best answer you can ever have. Then we’ll high five and I’ll give Rory a cuddle, tell him to have a good sleep and I’ll see him in the morning. Whiiiirrr. Funk. Tink.
